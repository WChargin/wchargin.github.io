Lessons from the Spire

TBD · view article source

Slay the Spire is a single-player strategy game. It’s a deck-building roguelike: play cards with different effects to defeat foes, and after each combat you’re offered the choice to add a card to your deck. If your health drops to zero, you die; you can start again on a new, randomly generated run.

Slay the Spire is a lovely game in its own right, but for me it goes deeper than that. Playing Spire has changed the way that I think about games, decisions, and the world. Here’s some of what I’ve learned.

Randomness

My thoughts on randomness as an element of game design have shifted dramatically. I’ll explain with an example.

In the beginning of a run of Spire, you start each turn by drawing 5 cards from your deck and gaining 3 energy. Playing cards costs energy. Cards typically cost around 1 energy, so you’ll have more cards in your hand than you can afford to play. Deciding which cards to play, and in which order, is the core of the tactical component of the game.

In some runs, you may be offered a relic called “Snecko Eye”. If you take Snecko Eye, you draw two additional cards per turn—7 total—but the cost of each card is randomized between 0 and 3 when drawn. Snecko Eye is often described as a “high variance” relic. You can have turns that are great, when you draw seven cards at low cost and can put powerful combos into play. And you can have turns where all your cards cost 3, and so you only get to play one of them.

Even a superficial analysis will show that Snecko Eye is, in general, a strong relic. It gives you more high-cost cards, but also more low-cost cards, so on average you can play about the same number of cards per turn. But you get to see seven cards!This gives you more flexibility to plan your turn, and lets you find critical cards in your deck more quickly.

Yet I used to dislike Snecko Eye quite a bit.
